A.1 — THE MOST BASIC MODEL: WHAT THE CODE DOES
====================================================================

GOAL
----
Train TransE on the PubMed Knowledge Graph and use the learned
embeddings to find a real paper X that is likely to cite works
similar to those cited by a chosen paper A.

DATASET
-------
PubMed is modelled as a KG with a single relation type:
    (paper_h, "cites", paper_t)
19,717 papers (nodes) and 44,338 citation links (edges).
The dataset is split into train (80%), validation (10%), test (10%)
with random_state=2026, ensuring reproducibility and stratification
(every entity appears at least once in the training set).

STEP 1 — Train TransE
---------------------
TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) is the simplest translational KGE model.
It learns one embedding vector per entity (paper) and one per relation.
The core assumption is:

    e(head) + e("cites") ≈ e(tail)

for every positive triple (head, "cites", tail) in the training set.
Training minimises the margin-based pairwise loss using negative
sampling: for each positive triple a corrupted triple is generated
by replacing the head or tail with a random entity.

Hyperparameters used:
    - embedding_dim = 128
    - num_epochs    = 100
    - batch_size    = 512
    - random_seed   = 2026

STEP 2 — Choose Paper A
-----------------------
We filter papers that have between 8 and 12 outgoing citations in
the training set. This range is chosen deliberately:
    - Too few citations (< 8): the mean embedding would not be
      representative enough.
    - Too many citations (> 12): the paper would be too general and
      the signal would be diluted.

Papers are sorted by their numeric index and the first one is selected.
Because the split uses random_state=2026 and the sorting is
deterministic, the same paper A is always selected across runs.

STEP 3 — Compute the TARGET embedding
--------------------------------------
Let B1, B2, ..., Bn be the papers cited by paper A.

From TransE:
    e(A) + e("cites") ≈ e(Bi)   for each i

Taking the mean over all cited papers:
    e(A) + e("cites") ≈ mean_cited      where mean_cited = mean(e(Bi))

Rearranging:
    TARGET = mean_cited - e("cites")

TARGET is the ideal embedding position of any paper that cites works
similar to those cited by A.

NOTE: in practice the cited papers do NOT collapse to a single point
in the embedding space because each paper Bi is also constrained by
many other triples in the KG (other papers that cite it, papers it
cites itself, etc.). TransE finds a global compromise. The limitation
of TransE is not literal collapse but reduced accuracy (lower Hits@K
and MRR) for one-to-many relations.

STEP 4 — Retrieve Paper X
--------------------------
Cosine similarity is computed between TARGET and every entity
embedding. Paper A itself is excluded. The paper with the highest
cosine similarity to TARGET is returned as paper X — the real paper
in the KG whose citation profile is most similar to that of paper A
according to the TransE model.

STEP 5 — 2D Visualisation
--------------------------
PCA (2 components) is applied to the set:
    {e(A), e(B1), ..., e(Bn), TARGET, e(X)}
The result is plotted, showing:
    - Paper A (blue)
    - Papers cited by A (gray, connected to A)
    - TARGET vector (orange star)
    - Paper X, the closest real paper (red)

====================================================================
A.2 — IMPROVING TransE: LIMITATION AND SOLUTION
====================================================================

(All models cited below are present in the Graph Representation
Learning theory slides provided for this assignment.)

LIMITATION OF TransE: ONE-TO-MANY RELATIONS
--------------------------------------------
TransE works well for simple 1-to-1 relations but struggles with
one-to-many relations. In our PubMed KG, the "cites" relation is
inherently one-to-many: a single paper can cite many different papers.

Concrete example using triples from the KG:

    (paper_A, cites, paper_B)
    (paper_A, cites, paper_C)
    (paper_A, cites, paper_D)

TransE must simultaneously satisfy:
    e(paper_A) + e("cites") ≈ e(paper_B)
    e(paper_A) + e("cites") ≈ e(paper_C)
    e(paper_A) + e("cites") ≈ e(paper_D)

Since e(paper_A) and e("cites") are fixed vectors, these three
equations are contradictory unless paper_B, paper_C and paper_D
occupy exactly the same position in the embedding space. In practice
they do not literally collapse (because other triples constrain them),
but TransE cannot minimise the loss to zero for all of them
simultaneously. This leads to higher residual error and lower link
prediction metrics (Hits@K, MRR) for one-to-many triples.

THE SOLUTION: TransH
--------------------
TransH (Wang et al., 2014) addresses this limitation by projecting
entities onto a relation-specific hyperplane before applying the
translation.

For each relation r, TransH learns:
    - w_r : the normal vector of the relation hyperplane
    - d_r : the translation vector within that hyperplane

The projected embeddings are:
    h_perp = h - w_r^T · h · w_r
    t_perp = t - w_r^T · t · w_r

The loss becomes:
    Loss = |(h_perp) + d_r - (t_perp)|

By projecting onto the hyperplane, the same entity can have different
effective positions for different relations or different roles within
the same relation. This allows paper_B, paper_C and paper_D to remain
at distinct positions in the full embedding space while still being
consistent with the "cites" relation of paper_A on the hyperplane.

WHY TransH IS APPROPRIATE FOR OUR KG
--------------------------------------
Since our KG contains only one relation ("cites") and that relation
is one-to-many by nature (papers typically cite multiple works),
TransH directly targets the core structural limitation of TransE
in this dataset. The additional expressiveness comes at a low cost:
two extra learned vectors per relation (w_r and d_r), which is
negligible given we have only one relation type.
